Google Search Central just dropped a concise set of tips for analyzing Search Console data more effectively. While the performance report is straightforward on the surface, the video reveals analytical techniques that most site owners are not using — techniques that transform raw numbers into strategic decisions.

Watch the full video: [Search Console data analysis tips](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CJExKneI2Vg)

## Why Most People Underuse Search Console

The typical Search Console workflow looks something like this: log in, glance at total clicks and impressions, maybe check if anything dropped dramatically, then close the tab. This is monitoring, not analysis. The difference between the two is the difference between knowing your traffic declined and knowing why it declined and what to do about it.

The video pushes site owners past the monitoring mindset and into analytical practices that produce actionable output. The core message is that Search Console data becomes powerful when you combine filters, compare time periods, and cross-reference dimensions.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Combine query and page filters to find mismatches.** Filter by a specific page, then examine which queries drive traffic to it. If a service page about "commercial HVAC repair" is getting traffic from "residential furnace maintenance," you have a content mismatch that is diluting relevance. Create a dedicated page for the residential query and tighten the commercial page's focus.

2. **Use date comparisons strategically around known events.** Do not just compare "last 28 days vs. previous 28 days." Compare the period before and after a specific event: a content update, a Google core update, a site migration, or a competitor launch. Targeted comparisons reveal causation, not just correlation.

3. **Filter by device to uncover mobile-specific issues.** A page that performs well on desktop but poorly on mobile may have rendering issues, slow load times, or layout problems that suppress mobile rankings. The device filter in Search Console is the fastest way to identify device-specific performance gaps.

4. **Sort by impressions to find untapped opportunities.** Pages with high impressions but low clicks have visibility but are not compelling users to click. These are your highest-leverage optimization targets. Improving the title tag and meta description for these pages can produce immediate traffic gains without requiring any content changes.

5. **Use the regex filter for advanced segmentation.** Search Console supports regular expression filtering for queries, which allows you to segment data by query patterns. Filter for queries containing "how to" to analyze your informational content performance. Filter for queries containing city names to assess local visibility. Filter for queries containing competitor brand names to understand competitive positioning.

## The Regex Advantage

The regex filtering capability deserves special attention because it unlocks analysis that is simply not possible with standard filters. Consider these practical applications:

Filter queries matching `(price|cost|how much)` to see how your site performs for commercial-intent searches. Filter queries matching `(best|top|review)` to assess your visibility for comparison searches. Filter queries matching your brand name to separate branded from non-branded traffic, which is essential for understanding whether your growth is driven by awareness or SEO.

These regex patterns let you create virtual segments within your Search Console data without exporting anything. For businesses tracking performance across multiple service lines, this is particularly valuable.

## The Export and Analyze Workflow

The video recommends exporting data for analysis that exceeds what the Search Console interface supports natively. Search Console caps the number of rows it displays, which means sites with thousands of queries or pages are only seeing a fraction of their data in the browser.

Exporting to Google Sheets or CSV lets you apply pivot tables, custom sorting, and conditional formatting to identify patterns that are invisible in the default interface. For example, you can group queries by intent category, calculate weighted CTR by position bracket, or identify query clusters where you rank on page two — the classic "striking distance" opportunities.

The video also suggests scheduling regular exports to build a historical dataset. Since Search Console only retains 16 months of data, monthly exports to a centralized spreadsheet or database ensure you have a complete record for year-over-year analysis.

## What This Means for Your Business

Search Console data is the closest thing to a direct line into how Google perceives your site. The businesses that extract the most value from it are not the ones with the most traffic — they are the ones with the most sophisticated analytical practices.

If you are only checking Search Console when something goes wrong, you are missing the proactive opportunities that the data reveals. High-impression, low-click pages are waiting for title tag optimization. Device-specific performance gaps are waiting to be closed. Query mismatches between pages are waiting to be resolved.
