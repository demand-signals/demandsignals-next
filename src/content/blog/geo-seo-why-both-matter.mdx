For the last two decades, "getting found online" meant one thing: ranking on Google. The entire discipline of search engine optimization was built on this single premise — understand how Google's algorithm works, build a site and content strategy that satisfies its signals, and customers will find you.

That model is still partially valid. But a parallel search ecosystem has emerged that operates on entirely different principles, and businesses that ignore it are leaving an increasing share of search traffic uncaptured.

The two disciplines are Search Engine Optimization (SEO) and Generative Engine Optimization (GEO). They are related but distinct. Understanding both — and building a strategy that covers both — is the competitive edge available right now, before most of your competitors figure it out.

## What Traditional SEO Actually Does

SEO is the practice of improving a website's visibility in traditional search engine results pages (SERPs). The core signals that Google uses have been documented, tested, and refined over decades:

- **Keyword relevance** — does your page content match what someone is searching for?
- **Authority signals** — how many high-quality sites link to yours?
- **Technical health** — is your site fast, mobile-friendly, and crawlable?
- **User behavior signals** — are people clicking your result and staying on your page?
- **Local signals** — for location-based queries, your Google Business Profile, citation consistency, and review volume matter enormously.

SEO works by securing positions in the ranked list of blue links. Position #1 receives roughly 27-28% of clicks for a given query. Position #10 receives under 3%. The entire game is about moving up that list.

## What Generative Engine Optimization (GEO) Does Differently

GEO addresses a different kind of search result entirely — the AI-generated summary. When someone asks ChatGPT "what are the best veterinarians in Folsom?" or asks Perplexity "who does kitchen remodels in Auburn, California?", they don't get a ranked list of blue links. They get a synthesized answer, often with two to four business citations woven into prose.

The signals that determine which businesses get cited in those answers are meaningfully different from traditional SEO signals:

- **Citation consistency** — is your NAP (name, address, phone) identical across every directory, social profile, and listing? Inconsistent citations confuse AI models and reduce citation probability.
- **Review volume and sentiment** — AI models heavily weight review data when forming local recommendations. A business with 200 reviews at 4.7 stars is far more likely to be cited than one with 12 reviews at 4.9 stars.
- **Structured data markup** — schema markup (LocalBusiness, FAQPage, Service schemas) makes your content machine-readable in a way that AI models can extract and synthesize.
- **Content authority on your topic** — AI models favor businesses that have substantial content demonstrating expertise in their category. A plumber with 40 articles about plumbing problems gets cited more than one with a five-page brochure site.
- **Third-party mentions** — local press, association memberships, directory listings, and social media profiles all contribute to the signal that an AI uses to validate your business as real, established, and trustworthy.

## The Three-Layer Approach: SEO + GEO + AEO

The most effective visibility strategy right now is a three-layer model that addresses traditional search, AI-generated answers, and voice/assistant queries simultaneously.

**Layer 1: SEO Foundation.** This is the baseline. A technically sound website, optimized on-page content, Google Business Profile management, and local citation building. Without this layer, layers two and three don't work as well — because SEO signals are inputs into GEO ranking too.

**Layer 2: GEO (Generative Engine Optimization).** Building citation authority, maintaining review velocity, implementing structured data markup, and creating content depth in your category. This is what gets you cited in ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Overviews.

**Layer 3: AEO (Answer Engine Optimization).** Structuring content to answer specific questions directly — FAQ pages, how-to guides, "near me" landing pages, and voice-search-friendly phrasing. This captures the growing share of zero-click and voice queries.

## Why Both SEO and GEO Matter Right Now

Here is the practical reality: Google's AI Overviews now appear on an estimated 20-35% of queries depending on category. Perplexity's user base has grown nearly tenfold since 2023. ChatGPT with web browsing is now a default search behavior for millions of users, especially in the 25-45 demographic.

At the same time, traditional Google organic search still drives the majority of referral traffic for most local businesses. Abandoning SEO in favor of GEO would be a mistake.

The window that is open right now — and it will close — is the early adopter advantage. Businesses that build GEO authority today, when most competitors have not even heard the term, will be the default citations when AI models synthesize answers for their category.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

A local HVAC company operating in the SEO-only model might rank #3 for "HVAC repair Sacramento" on Google. In the GEO model, they also appear in the AI Overview that shows up above the ranked results for that same query, and they get cited by Perplexity when someone asks "who does emergency AC repair in Sacramento?"

That is triple visibility from a unified strategy: organic rank, AI Overview citation, and Perplexity citation. The cost to achieve all three is only marginally higher than the cost of achieving one.
